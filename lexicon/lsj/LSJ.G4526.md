
<b> <span class='asgreek'>==σάκκος==</span></b> 


 or <b><span class='asgreek'>σάκος</span></b>, <span class='asgreek'>ὁ</span>, see entry near the end:— <b>coarse cloth of hair</b>, especially of goats' hair, <span class='asgreek'>==σάκκος==</span> <span class='asgreek'>τρίχινος</span> [<a href="javascript:void(0)" title=" NT.Rev.6.12, cf. LXX.Isa.50.3, LXX.Sir.25.17. ">LXX+NT</a>]

$\quad$ $\quad$<b>\_\_II</b> <b>anything made of this cloth</b>: 
$\quad$ $\quad$ $\quad$<b>\_\_II.1</b> <b>sack, bag</b>, [<a href="javascript:void(0)" title=" 5th c.BC: Herodotus Historicus 9.80, 5th-6th c.BC: Aristophanes Comicus “Acharnenses” 745, “Lys.” 1209, 2nd c.AD: Galenus Medicus 2.559,8.672:—as a measure, “Ostr.” 1096, +others ">Refs 5th c.BC+</a>]
$\quad$ $\quad$ $\quad$<b>\_\_II.2</b> <b>sieve, strainer</b>, especially for wine, [<a href="javascript:void(0)" title=" 6th c.BC: Hipponax Iambographus 57, 2nd c.AD(?): Pollianus Epigrammaticus 6.19; σ. τρίχινοι “PHamb.” 10.39 (2nd c.AD). ">Refs 6th c.BC+</a>]
$\quad$ $\quad$ $\quad$<b>\_\_II.3</b> <b>coarse garment, sackcloth</b>, worn as mourning by the Jews, [<a href="javascript:void(0)" title=" LXX.Gen.37.34, NT.Luke.10.13, 1st c.AD: Josephus Historicus “Bellum Judaicum” 2.12.5, compare 1st-2nd c.AD: Plutarchus Biographus et Philosophus 2.239c. ">LXX+NT+1st c.AD+</a>]
$\quad$ $\quad$<b>\_\_III</b> <b>coarse beard</b>, like rough hair-cloth, <span class='asgreek'>σάκον</span> <span class='asgreek'>πρὸς</span> <span class='asgreek'>ταῖν</span> <span class='asgreek'>γνάθοιν</span> <span class='asgreek'>ἔχειν</span> [<a href="javascript:void(0)" title=" 5th-6th c.BC: Aristophanes Comicus “Ecclesiazusae” 502; compare σακεσφόρος 11.">Refs 5th c.BC+</a>] —The form <span class='asgreek'>σάκος</span> is said to be <i>Attic dialect</i>, [<a href="javascript:void(0)" title=" 2nd c.AD: Aelius Dionysius Grammaticus “fragment” 296, 2nd c.AD: Phrynichus Atticista 229, 2nd c.AD: Moeris Grammaticus p.354 P., 13th-14th c.AD: Thomas Magister Grammaticus p.344 ">Refs 2nd c.AD+</a>] R., etc.; while <span class='asgreek'>==σάκκος==</span> is called <i>Doric dialect</i> by [<a href="javascript:void(0)" title=" 2nd c.AD: Phrynichus Atticista [same place], Hellenic by 2nd c.AD: Moeris Grammaticus and 13th-14th c.AD: Thomas Magister Grammaticus [same places], Comic by 2nd c.AD(?): Pollianus Epigrammaticus 7.191. In 5th-6th c.BC: Aristophanes Comicus “Acharnenses” 822, “Ec.” 502, σάκος is required by the metre, as is ==σάκκος== in “Ach.” 745 (from Megarian), and in 6th c.BC: Hipponax Iambographus [same place]; codices of 5th c.BC: Herodotus Historicus give ==σάκκος==">Refs 6th c.BC+</a>]. Inscrr. have <span class='asgreek'>σάκος</span> [<a href="javascript:void(0)" title=" “IG” 22.1672.73,74, 108 and ==σάκκος== [prev. work] 198">Refs</a>]: Papyri have <span class='asgreek'>σάκος</span> [<a href="javascript:void(0)" title=" “PCair.Zen.” 753.27 (3rd c.BC), “UPZ” 84.52 (2nd c.BC)">Refs 3rd c.BC+</a>], but oftener <span class='asgreek'>==σάκκος==</span> [<a href="javascript:void(0)" title=" “PSI” 4.427.1, 14 (3rd c.BC), “PTeb.” 116.3 (2nd c.BC)">Refs 3rd c.BC+</a>], etc. (Prob. the word, like the thing, was borrowed from Phoenicia, cf. Hebrew <b>saq.</b>)
