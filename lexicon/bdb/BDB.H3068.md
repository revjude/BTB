 <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎יהוה‎</span><sub>c. 6823</sub> i.e. <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎יַהְוֶה‎</span> **proper name, of deity** ==Yahweh==, the proper name of the God of Israel — (

**1.** [[BDB#MT|ᵑ0]] <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎יְהוָֺה‎</span><sup>C518</sup> ([[BDB#Qr|Qr]] <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎אֲדֹנָי‎</span>), or <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎יֱהוִֺה‎</span><sub>305</sub> ([[BDB#Qr|Qr]] <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎אֱלֹהִים‎</span>), in the combinations <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎אדני יהוה‎</span> &amp; <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎יהוה אדני‎</span> (see <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎אֲדֹנֶי‎</span>), and with preposition <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎בַּיהוָֺה‎</span>, <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎לַיהוָֺה‎</span>, <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎מֵיהוָֺה‎</span> ([[BDB#Qr|Qr]] <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎בַּאדֹנָי‎</span>, <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎לַאדֹנָי‎</span>, <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎מֵאדֹנָי‎</span>), do not give the original form. [[BDB#Sept|ᵐ5]] and other [[BDB#Vrss|Vrss]] follow the [[BDB#Qr|Qr]]. On the basis of [[Exod.20.7|Exod 20:7]]; [[Lev.24.11|Lev 24:11]] <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎יהוה‎</span> was regarded as a ***nomen ineffabile*** (see Philo<sup>de Vita Mosis iii. 519, 529</sup>), called by the Jews <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎הַשֵּׁם‎</span> and by the Samaritans <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎שׁימא‎</span>. The pronunciation ***Jehovah*** was unknown until 1520, when it was introduced by Galatinus; but it was contested by Le Mercier, J. Drusius, and L. Capellus, as against grammatical and historical propriety (compare [[BDB#Bö|Bö]]<sup>§ 88</sup>). The traditional <span class="bdbLangGreek">Ἰαβέ</span> of Theodoret and Epiphanius, the <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎יָֿהוּ‎</span>, <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎יְהוֺֿ‎</span> of compound proper name and the contracted form <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎יָהּ‎</span> all favour <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎יַהְוֶךְ‎</span> (compare <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎יַהֲלֹמ֑וּן‎</span> [[Ps.74.6|Ps 74:6]] <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎תַּהֲרוּ‎</span> [[Isa.33.11|Isa 33:11]]), see [[BDB#Lag|Lag]]<sup>Sym i.14</sup> [[BDB#Bau|Baud]]<sup>Studien i.179 ff.</sup>; [[BDB#Dr|Dr]]<sup>Stud.Bib.i.1 ff.</sup> For ***Jeve*** see [[BDB#Sta|Sta]]<sup>ZAW 1881, 346</sup> [[BDB#De|De]]<sup>ib.1882, 173 f. &amp; Gn. Excurs. ii.</sup>

**I.** <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎יהוה‎</span> is not used by [[BDB#E|E]] in Genesis, but is given <ref ref="Exod 3:12-15" b="2" cBegin="3" vBegin="12" cEnd="3" vEnd="15" onclick="bcv(2,3,12)">Exod 3:12-15</ref> as the name of the God who revealed Himself to Moses at Horeb, and is explained thus : <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎אֶהְיֶה עִמָּ֑ךְ‎</span> ***I shall be with thee*** ([[Exod.3.12|Exod 3:12]]), which is then implied in <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎אֶהְיֶה אֲשֶׁר אֶהְיֶה‎</span> ***I shall be the one who will be*** it v[[Exod.3.14|Exod 3:14]]a (i.e: with thee v[[Exod.3.12|Exod 3:12]]) and then compressed into <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎אֶהְיֶה‎</span> v[[Exod.3.14|Exod 3:14]]b (i.e. with thee v[[Exod.3.12|Exod 3:12]]), which then is given in the nominal form <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎יהוה‎</span> ***He who will be*** it v[[Exod.3.15|Exod 3:15]] (i.e. with thee v[[Exod.3.12|Exod 3:12]]). compare [[BDB#Ew|Ew]]<sup>BTh ii. 337, 338</sup> [[BDB#RS|RS]]<sup>l.c., Proph. 385 ff.</sup> Other interpretations are: ***I am he who I am***, i.e. it is no concern of yours (Le Clerc [[BDB#Lag|Lag]]<sup>Psalt.Hieron.156</sup>); ***I am*** (this is my name), ***inasmuch as I am*** (<span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎אֲשֶׁר‎</span> = <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎כִּי‎</span>; [[BDB#AE|AE]] [[BDB#JDMich|JDMich]] [[BDB#We|We]]<sup>JD</sup> [[BDB#The|Th]] xxi, 540 = compare Hexateuch 72); [[BDB#Di|Di]] and others ***I am who I am***, he who is essentially unnameable, inexplicable, — [[BDB#E|E]] uses <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎יהוה‎</span> sparingly by the side of <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎אלהים‎</span> and <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎האלהים‎</span> in his subsequent narrative. The Ephraimitic writers in Judges Samuel Kings use it in similar proportions. [[BDB#P|P]] abstains from the use of <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎יהוה‎</span> until he gives an account of its revelation to Moses [[Exod.6.3|Exod 6:3]] but subsequently uses it freely. He gives no explanation of its meaning. He represents that <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎אֵל שַׁדַּי‎</span> was the God of the patriarchs. [[BDB#J|J]] uses <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎יהוה‎</span> from the beginning of his narrative, possibly explaining it, [[Gen.21.83|Gen 21:83]] by <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎אל‎</span> <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎עולם‎</span>, the evergreen tamarisk being a symbol of the ever-living God; compare [[BDB#De|De]] [[Gen.21.33|Gen 21:33]]. Elsewhere <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎יהוה‎</span> is the common divine name in pre-exilic writers, but in post-exilic writers gradually falls into disuse, and is supplanted by <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎אלהים‎</span> and <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎אדני‎</span>. In Job it is used 31 t. in prose parts, and [[Job.12.9|Job 12:9]] (a proverb); not elsewhere in the poem. Chronicles apart from his sources prefers <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎אלהים‎</span> and <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎האלהים‎</span>. Daniel uses <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎יהוה‎</span> only in chap. 9 (7 t.); Ecclesiastes not at all. In the Elohistic group of Psalm 42-83 it is used 39 t. (see <span class="bdbLangHebrew">‎אלהים‎</span>) . It occurs as the name of Israel's God [[BDB#MI|MI]]<sup>18</sup>. It is doubtful whether it was used by other branches of the Shemitic family, compare [[BDB#COT|COT]] [[Gen.2.4|Gen 2:4]]b [[BDB#Dl|Dl]]<sup>Pa 158 ff.</sup> [[BDB#Dr|Dr]]<sup>Stud. Bib. i. 7 ff</sup>.
