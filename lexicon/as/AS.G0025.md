<span class="asform"><span class="asgreek"><span class="asbold">ἀγαπάω</span></span>, <span class="asgreek">-ῶ</span>, </span> <span class="asetym">  <span class="asseptuagint">\[in LXX chiefly for **<big>[[H0157|אהב]]</big>**;\]</span> </span>
<span class="asitalic">to love</span>, to feel and exhibit esteem and goodwill to a person, to prize and delight in a thing.
<span class="asbullet"><span class="asbold">1.</span> Of human affection, to men: <span class="asgreek">τ. πλησίον</span>, [[Matt.5.43|Mt 5:43]]; <span class="asgreek">τ. ἐχθρούς</span>, [[Matt.5.44|ib. 44]]; to Christ, [[John.8.42|Jo 8:42]]; to God, [[Matt.22.37|Mt 22:37]]; c. acc. rei, [[Luke.11.43|Lk 11:43]], [[John.12.43|Jo 12:43]], [[Eph.5.25|Eph 5:25]], [[2Tim.4.8|II Tim 4:8]], [[2Tim.4.10|10]], [[Heb.1.9|He 1:9]], [[1Pet.2.17|I Pe 2:17]], [[1Pet.3.10|3:10]], [[2Pet.2.15|II Pe 2:15]], [[1John.2.15|I Jo 2:15]], [[Rev.12.11|Re 12:11]].</span>
<span class="asbullet"><span class="asbold">2.</span> Of divine love;</span>
$\quad$ <span class="asbullet"><span class="asbold">(a)</span> God's love: to men, [[Rom.8.37|Ro 8:37]]; to Christ, [[John.3.35|Jo 3:35]];</span>
$\quad$ <span class="asbullet"><span class="asbold">(b)</span> Christ's love: to men, [[Mark.10.21|Mk 10:21]]; to God, [[John.14.31|Jo 14:31]]; c. cogn. acc., [[John.17.26|Jo 17:26]], [[Eph.2.4|Eph 2:4]].</span>
<span class="asre"><span class="asitalic">SYN.</span>: <span class="asref"><span class="asgreek">φιλέω</span></span>. From its supposed etymology (Thayer, LS; but v. also Boisacq) <span class="asgreek">ἀ.</span> is commonly understood properly to denote love based on esteem (<span class="aslatin">diligo</span>), as distinct from that expressed by <span class="asref"><span class="asgreek">φιλέω</span></span> (<span class="aslatin">amo</span>), spontaneous natural affection, emotional and unreasoning. If this distinction holds, <span class="asgreek">ἀ.</span> is fitly used in NT of Christian love to God and man, the spiritual affection which follows the direction of the will, and which, therefore, unlike that feeling which is instinctive and unreasoned, can be commanded as a duty. (Cf. <span class="asref"><span class="asgreek">ἀγάπη</span></span>, and <span class="asitalic">v</span>. Tr., <span class="asitalic">Syn.</span> §xii; Cremer, 9, 592; and esp. MM, <span class="asitalic">VGT</span>, s.v.) </span>
