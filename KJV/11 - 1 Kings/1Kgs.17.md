$\quad$ <span class="startversemarker">[</span>**[[1Kgs.17.17|17]]**<span class="endversemarker">]</span> And it came to pass after these things, \[that\] the son of the woman<small><small>[^36]</small></small>, the mistress of the house, fell sick; and his sickness was so sore, that there was<small><small>[^37]</small></small> no breath left in him.<small><small>[^38]</small></small> <span class="startversemarker">[</span>**[[1Kgs.17.18|18]]**<span class="endversemarker">]</span> And she said unto Elijah, What have I<small><small>[^39]</small></small> to do with thee, O thou man<small><small>[^40]</small></small> of God? art thou come<small><small>[^41]</small></small> unto me to call my sin to remembrance, and to slay my son? <span class="startversemarker">[</span>**[[1Kgs.17.19|19]]**<span class="endversemarker">]</span> And he said unto her, Give me thy son. And he took him out of her bosom, and carried him up into a loft<small><small>[^42]</small></small>, where he abode, and laid him upon his own bed. <span class="startversemarker">[</span>**[[1Kgs.17.20|20]]**<span class="endversemarker">]</span> And he cried<small><small>[^43]</small></small> unto the <span class="smallcaps">Lord</span>, and said, O <span class="smallcaps">Lord</span> my God, hast thou also<small><small>[^44]</small></small> brought evil upon the widow with whom I sojourn, by slaying her son? <span class="startversemarker">[</span>**[[1Kgs.17.21|21]]**<span class="endversemarker">]</span> And he stretched himself<small><small>[^45]</small></small> upon the child three times, and cried unto the <span class="smallcaps">Lord</span>, and said, O <span class="smallcaps">Lord</span> my God<small><small>[^46]</small></small>, I pray thee, let this child’s soul come into him again. <span class="startversemarker">[</span>**[[1Kgs.17.22|22]]**<span class="endversemarker">]</span> And the <span class="smallcaps">Lord</span> heard the voice of Elijah; and the soul of the child came into him<small><small>[^47]</small></small> again, and he revived<small><small>[^48]</small></small>. <span class="startversemarker">[</span>**[[1Kgs.17.23|23]]**<span class="endversemarker">]</span> And Elijah took the child, and brought him down out of the chamber into the house, and delivered him unto his mother: and Elijah said, See, thy son liveth<small><small>[^49]</small></small>.

[^10]: [[1Kgs.19|1Kgs 19:9]]; [[Prov.3|Prov 3:5]]; [[Matt.16|Matt 16:24]]; [[John.15|John 15:14]]; Many learned men have raised doubts on those parts of the Inspired Word, which may, by the perverseness of their argument and the ingenuity of their surmise, be made to appear inconsistent with fact. In this case, they are not satisfied with being expressly told by God that the ravens supplied Elijah with food, while the brook gave him drink, but apparently to mystify a manifest miracle, they suggest whether these ravens might not be merchantmen, or the inhabitants of a neighbouring town. Let any unprejudiced reader and lover of the Bible take the whole history of Elijah, and he will find that his life was almost a daily illustration of the power of God in his miraculous interpositions. Instance the supply of provision in the unwasting barrel of meal and cruse of oil, after the prophet had removed to Zarephath. The power communicated to him to raise the widow's son from death. The wonderful interposition of the Lord to prove the folly of Baal's worshippers, in sending down fire from heaven to consume Elijah's sacrifice and lick up the water, although the sacrifice had been saturated therewith, and the altar surrounded by a deep trench to prevent its running away. The prayer for rain is another instance. The sojourn in Horeb forty days and forty nights, after having eaten of the cake: the destruction of Ahaziah's messengers twice. The smiting of the waters at Jordan. The fall of the mantle on Elisha, and finally, in the closing scene of life, he was taken to glory without tasting the pains of death, the sting was taken away.
